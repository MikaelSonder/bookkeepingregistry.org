Executive Summary

The UGB requires certain businesses to keep books and prepare accounts, including capital companies which are always obliged and other businesses that cross turnover or value thresholds; free professions are generally exempt from UGB bookkeeping but follow income tax rules.

The BAO and related tax rules set the general obligation to retain books, records and supporting documents for seven years, starting from the end of the calendar year to which the bookkeeping entry or document relates, with longer periods for real estate‑related records and other special situations.

Austrian businesses must keep inventories, opening balance sheets, annual financial statements, management reports, accounts, vouchers, business papers and income and expense records and may retain them electronically if faithful, complete and readable throughout the retention period.

A separate business account is strongly recommended and usually expected for legal entities, because it underpins clean VAT and tax records and makes it easier to comply with the BAO’s seven‑year retention and documentary standards.

Purpose

The purpose of Austrian bookkeeping is to provide a reliable basis for statutory financial statements and tax returns, ensure compliance with UGB and BAO obligations and allow authorities, owners and creditors to assess the financial position and performance of the business.

Primary Outcome

Properly maintained Austrian books and records retained for at least seven years, and longer where required, that comply with UGB and BAO rules and support financial reporting and tax audits.

Regulatory & Operational Framework

Framework summarises key rule layers for Austrian bookkeeping and accounting.

Unternehmensgesetzbuch (UGB) Defines which businesses must keep books, prepare accounts and follow double‑entry accounting.
Bundesabgabenordnung (BAO) Sets general record‑keeping obligations and retention periods, including seven‑year standard retention.
VAT Law Defines VAT record requirements and interacts with BAO retention obligations.
Process Flow

Process flow explains how Austrian bookkeeping typically progresses from transactions to reporting and retention.

1. Determine Bookkeeping Obligation Assess whether the business must keep books under UGB or may rely on income and expense records.
2. Set Up Books and Accounts Configure double‑entry books or income records and establish a separate business bank account.
3. Record Transactions Record all income, expenses, assets, liabilities and other relevant events with supporting documents.
4. Prepare Financial Statements and Tax Returns Use records to prepare annual accounts and tax returns, including VAT declarations.
5. Archive Records Retain books, records and vouchers for at least seven years, and longer for real estate and special cases.
Decision Tree

Decision tree simplifies key questions that determine the Austrian bookkeeping route.

  1. Is the business obliged to keep books under UGB based on legal form or thresholds?
  2. Are records kept in a way that meets BAO requirements for completeness, authenticity and legibility?
  3. Is VAT treatment and any Kleinunternehmer exemption properly documented?
  4. Do retention practices cover seven years and longer periods for real estate and ongoing proceedings?

Operating Constraints Risks

Operating constraints highlight recurring risks in Austrian bookkeeping practice.

Retention Risk Destroying records before seven years or before the end of extended periods for property or proceedings can breach statutory obligations and weaken defence in audits.
Documentation Risk Incomplete or illegible records, including poorly managed digital archives, may fail BAO standards.

FAQ

FAQ summarises recurring threshold questions related to Austrian bookkeeping.

Must All Businesses Keep Books? No. Bookkeeping obligations depend on legal form and thresholds; some small businesses and free professions use income and expense records instead.
How Long Are Records Kept? Generally seven years, with longer periods for real estate‑related documents and ongoing proceedings.
Can Records Be Kept Digitally? Yes, provided electronic records remain complete, faithful and readable throughout the retention period.
