Executive Summary

The Hungarian Accounting Act defines reporting and bookkeeping obligations, principles to be followed when compiling reports and the framework for financial statements.[web:320][web:321]

Businesses must prepare financial statements in accordance with Act C of 2000, ensuring a true and fair view of their financial position and performance, and are required to use double-entry bookkeeping systems.[web:321][web:328][web:333]

Accounting records must be kept in legible form for at least eight years, and documents directly or indirectly supporting bookkeeping (including receipts) must be stored readably and retrievable by reference to the records for the same period.[web:326][web:327][web:333]

Tax rules generally require documents to be kept until the limitation period of tax assessment (usually five years from the end of the year of filing), but if different rules apply to the same document, the longer archiving period must be respected.[web:327]

Purpose

The purpose of Hungarian bookkeeping is to provide reliable financial information for owners, creditors, tax authorities and regulators and to serve as the basis for statutory financial statements and tax calculations.[web:321][web:322][web:333]

It converts economic events into structured double-entry records that can be audited and analysed.[web:320][web:328]

Primary Outcome

Properly maintained Hungarian double-entry books and eight‑year archives of accounting records and supporting documents that allow true and fair financial statements and tax compliance.[web:321][web:326][web:327][web:333]
