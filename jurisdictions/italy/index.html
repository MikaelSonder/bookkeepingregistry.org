Executive Summary

Every Italian company is legally required to maintain accounting records that clearly document all financial transactions and to file annual accounts with the Registrar of Companies within statutory deadlines.[web:194][web:195][web:196][web:197]

Italian companies generally follow Italian GAAP (OIC) for statutory reporting, while listed companies and large groups must adopt IFRS as endorsed by the EU for consolidated financial statements.[web:195][web:196][web:202]

Standard bookkeeping requires a journal, inventory book, VAT registers and a fixed assets register, together with corporate books such as shareholders’ and minutes books, with records maintained in Italian and retained for at least ten years.[web:195][web:199][web:200][web:204]

Accounting books and tax documents must be kept for at least ten years from the recording date under the Civil Code, while VAT documentation is typically retained for at least five years; digital bookkeeping and electronic archiving are permitted but subject to specific rules that were simplified in 2022.[web:195][web:199][web:201][web:205]

Purpose

The purpose of bookkeeping in Italy is to provide a precise and organised view of the company’s economic and financial situation, support statutory accounts and enable tax and regulatory authorities to verify transactions and results.[web:198][web:194][web:195]

It translates legal and commercial obligations into traceable accounting records and books that carry civil and tax evidential value.[web:199][web:201][web:204]

Primary Outcome

Properly maintained Italian accounting books and records that meet civil and tax requirements, support GAAP or IFRS financial statements and are retained for the required legal periods.[web:194][web:195][web:196][web:199][web:201][web:204]

Country Characteristics

Country characteristics explain how Italian bookkeeping is shaped by law, standards and practice.[web:195][web:196][web:199][web:200][web:201][web:204][web:205]

Standards Orientation Italian GAAP (OIC) governs statutory accounts; IFRS applies to listed companies and large groups for consolidated reports, while small entities cannot adopt IFRS for statutory accounts.[web:195][web:196][web:202]
Mandatory Books Structure Journal, inventory book, VAT registers and fixed assets register form the core; corporate books record governance decisions.[web:195][web:199][web:201][web:204]
Retention Orientation Ten-year retention for accounting and tax documents under Civil Code Article 2220, with five-year focus for VAT but practical preference for ten-year horizon.[web:199][web:201][web:204]
Digital Archiving Simplification Obligation to preserve accounting books electronically using e‑invoice archiving rules has been withdrawn; it is now sufficient to keep books updated in IT systems and print them on request.[web:205]

Regulatory & Operational Framework

Framework summarises key rule layers for Italian bookkeeping.[web:194][web:195][web:196][web:199][web:201][web:203][web:204]

Civil Code and Company Law Require keeping accounting books that identify all transactions, preparing and approving financial statements and filing accounts with the Company Register.[web:194][web:196][web:199][web:204]
Italian GAAP (OIC) Defines accounting principles and presentation for statutory accounts; small and medium companies may use abbreviated formats under Article 2435‑bis.[web:195][web:196][web:202]
IFRS Adoption Mandatory for listed companies and large groups for consolidated reports; not available to small entities for statutory accounts.[web:195][web:196]
VAT and Tax Regulations Define VAT registers, fixed assets registers and retention rules for tax documents.[web:195][web:199][web:203][web:204]
Archiving Rules Simplification Simplifying Law Decree 73/2022 removed strict electronic preservation obligations, allowing up‑to‑date computer records and printing on request.[web:205]
Process Flow

Process flow explains how Italian bookkeeping progresses from transaction recording to annual accounts and archiving.[web:195][web:199][web:200][web:201][web:204][web:207]

1. Configure Books and Standards Decide whether Italian GAAP or IFRS applies, set up chart of accounts and open journal and inventory books.[web:195][web:196][web:201][web:204]
2. Record Daily Operations Use the journal (daybook) to record daily operations and company transactions, supported by vouchers.[web:195][web:199][web:201]
3. Maintain Inventory and Fixed Assets Update inventory book annually and maintain fixed assets register for tax and accounting.[web:195][web:199][web:201][web:204]
4. Manage VAT Registers Keep registers of issued invoices, purchase invoices and retailers’ considerations for VAT rules.[web:195][web:199][web:201][web:203][web:204]
5. Prepare Annual Accounts Prepare balance sheet, income statement, notes and, where required, management report, then obtain shareholder approval.[web:194][web:195][web:196]
6. File Annual Accounts File accounts electronically with the Company Register within legal deadlines; accounts become publicly accessible.[web:194][web:195][web:196]
7. Archive Books and Documents Retain accounting books and tax documents for at least ten years, in digital or paper form, in line with updated archiving rules.[web:199][web:201][web:204][web:205]

FAQ

FAQ summarises recurring threshold questions related to Italian bookkeeping.[web:194][web:195][web:196][web:199][web:201][web:204][web:205]

Must All Companies Maintain Accounting Records? Yes. Every Italian company must keep accounting records sufficient to identify all financial transactions.[web:194][web:195][web:197]
Which Standards Apply? Italian GAAP (OIC) for statutory accounts; IFRS for listed and large groups for consolidated reporting.[web:195][web:196][web:202]
How Long Are Books Retained? Accounting books and tax documents are typically retained for at least ten years; VAT documents for at least five.[web:199][web:201][web:204]
Is Electronic Preservation Still Obligatory? No. 2022 simplification removed the obligation to preserve books electronically following e‑invoice rules; up‑to‑date computer records and printing on request are sufficient.[web:205]
What Happens if Books Are Incorrect? Improperly maintained books can lead to administrative penalties per book, plus tax consequences.[web:199]
