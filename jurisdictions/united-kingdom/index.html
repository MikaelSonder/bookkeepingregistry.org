Systematic recording of UK business transactions in accounting systems that provide adequate records for statutory accounts under UK GAAP or IFRS and for HMRC tax compliance.

Scope

Covered Matters Company and accounting record obligations, UK GAAP and IFRS options, preparation and filing of annual accounts at Companies House, HMRC record‑keeping rules and six‑year retention, VAT and payroll records and extended retention for special schemes and compliance checks.

Executive Summary

UK companies must keep records about the company itself (such as shareholders, loans and security) and adequate accounting records that show all money received and spent, assets, debts, stock and all goods bought and sold and must keep business finances separate from the personal finances of owners and directors.[web:211]

Accounting records must be sufficient to prepare annual accounts that show a true and fair view and to support Company Tax Returns; failing to keep records can result in fines or disqualification as a director.[web:211]

HMRC generally requires accounting records to be kept for six years from the end of the accounting period or financial year they relate to, with longer retention where records relate to long‑term assets, multi‑period transactions, late returns or ongoing compliance checks; VAT and payroll records have specific minimum periods, and certain EU‑related VAT schemes require ten‑year retention.[web:431][web:432][web:434]

UK entities prepare accounts under UK GAAP (FRS 101, FRS 102, FRS 105) or IFRS as adopted by the UK; bookkeeping provides the underlying records for these financial statements and for filings with Companies House and HMRC.[web:209]

Purpose

The purpose of UK bookkeeping is to provide a reliable basis for statutory accounts and tax returns, enable regulators and stakeholders to assess the company’s financial position and ensure compliance with UK company and tax law.[web:211][web:209]

Primary Outcome

Adequate UK accounting and company records retained for at least six years (and longer where required) that support true‑and‑fair financial statements, Companies House filings and HMRC compliance.[web:211][web:431][web:432]

Regulatory & Operational Framework

Framework summarises key rule layers for UK bookkeeping and accounting.[web:211][web:431][web:433][web:209]

Companies Act 2006 Defines requirements to keep accounting records and minimum preservation periods for private and public companies.[web:433]
HMRC Compliance Handbook Sets six‑year retention norms for company tax records and specific periods for VAT records.[web:431][web:437]
UK GAAP and IFRS Framework Provides accounting standards (FRS 101, 102, 105 and IFRS) under which statutory accounts are prepared.[web:209]
Process Flow

Process flow explains how UK bookkeeping typically progresses from transactions to reporting and retention.[web:211][web:209]

1. Set Up Company Records and Systems Establish company registers and accounting systems that record all income, expenditure, assets, debts and stock.[web:211][web:209]
2. Record Transactions Record money received and spent, goods bought and sold and other financial events with appropriate supporting documents.[web:211]
3. Prepare Statutory Accounts Prepare annual accounts under UK GAAP or IFRS that show a true and fair view and file them at Companies House.[web:209][web:435]
4. File Tax Returns Use bookkeeping records to prepare Company Tax Returns, VAT returns and payroll submissions to HMRC.[web:211][web:431]
5. Retain Records Store company and accounting records for at least six years and longer where required by HMRC rules or business needs.[web:211][web:431][web:432]

FAQ

FAQ summarises recurring threshold questions related to UK bookkeeping.[web:211][web:430][web:432]

Must All Companies Keep Accounting Records? Yes. UK companies must keep adequate accounting records and records about the company.[web:211]
How Long Are Records Kept? Most accounting records are kept for at least six years from the end of the financial year, with longer periods for some VAT schemes and special circumstances.[web:211][web:431][web:432][web:434]
What Happens If Records Are Lost? Companies must do their best to recreate them, inform HMRC and note the loss in their tax returns.[web:211]
