Executive Summary

The Swiss Code of Obligations fixes the regulations on proper accounting, requiring an inventory, a full balance sheet and a profit and loss statement based on supporting documents; legal entities and larger sole proprietorships and partnerships must draw up and present accounts that comply with these rules.

Smaller sole proprietorships and partnerships with less than CHF 500,000 turnover may use simplified accounting based on income, expenses and assets but are still subject to retention rules for business documents.

Article 958f of the Code of Obligations requires business books, accounting vouchers, business reports and audit reports to be retained for ten years from the end of the financial year; tax and VAT law extend this in some cases, with certain real property and VAT input tax correction records requiring up to twenty years’ retention.

Accounting books and records may be kept on paper or electronically; the GeBüV ordinance sets conditions for electronic storage, distinguishing non‑modifiable data carriers from modifiable ones, which must use technical procedures such as electronic signatures and time stamps to ensure integrity and authenticity.

Purpose

The purpose of Swiss bookkeeping is to provide a reliable basis for statutory financial reporting and tax returns, enable authorities and stakeholders to assess the financial position and performance and ensure traceability and integrity of business transactions over the legally required retention periods.

Primary Outcome

Properly maintained Swiss accounting books and business documents retained for at least ten years (and longer for specific VAT and property records) that comply with the Code of Obligations, GeBüV and tax law and support audits and compliance checks.

Regulatory & Operational Framework

Framework summarises key rule layers for Swiss bookkeeping and accounting.

Swiss Code of Obligations (CO) Articles 957 et seq. define compulsory accounting and proper accounting; Article 958f sets ten‑year retention.
GeBüV Ordinance Defines principles for keeping and retaining accounting records and permitted information carriers.
Tax and VAT Law Extend retention for some documents and require records until final expiry of tax claims, with longer periods for specific property‑related VAT corrections.
Process Flow

Process flow explains how Swiss bookkeeping typically progresses from transactions to reporting and retention.

1. Determine Accounting Regime Assess whether full proper accounting or simplified accounting applies based on legal form and turnover.
2. Set Up Books and Records Configure business books, accounting vouchers and supporting documentation, choosing paper or electronic formats within GeBüV rules.
3. Record Transactions Record all business transactions with documented evidence that allows auditability and traceability.
4. Prepare Annual and Audit Reports Prepare annual report, financial statements and, where required, audit report in written and signed form.
5. Archive Records Retain books, vouchers and reports for ten years (or longer where tax or VAT rules require) in compliant storage systems.
Decision Tree

Decision tree simplifies key questions that determine the Swiss bookkeeping route.

  1. Is the entity subject to compulsory accounting under the Code of Obligations or eligible for simplified accounting?
  2. Are accounting records sufficient to produce inventory, balance sheet and profit and loss statement?
  3. Are records stored in formats and systems that meet GeBüV integrity and accessibility requirements?
  4. Do retention schedules cover ten years and longer periods for specific tax and VAT obligations?

Operating Constraints Risks

Operating constraints highlight recurring risks in Swiss bookkeeping practice.

Retention Risk Destroying records before ten years or before tax claims have expired can breach obligations and weaken defence in audits; some documents require up to twenty years.
Electronic Integrity Risk Using modifiable electronic storage without robust integrity safeguards may fail GeBüV requirements.

FAQ

FAQ summarises recurring threshold questions related to Swiss bookkeeping.

Must All Businesses Keep Proper Accounts? Legal entities and larger sole proprietorships and partnerships must; smaller entities use simplified accounting.
How Long Are Business Records Retained? Generally ten years from financial year-end, with longer periods for certain VAT and property‑related documents.
Can Cloud Storage Be Used? Yes, but treated as modifiable data and subject to stricter integrity and authenticity requirements.
