Executive Summary

Spanish bookkeeping is anchored in the Commercial Code and the General Accounting Plan (PGC), which require trading companies to keep accounting books, legalise them at the Mercantile Registry and prepare annual accounts that show a true and fair view of assets, financial situation and results.[web:181][web:182][web:187][web:190][web:191]

Mandatory books include the journal and the Inventory Book and Annual Accounts, opened with an initial balance sheet and containing quarterly trial balances, closing inventories and annual accounts; the general ledger is widely used in practice to track movements by account.[web:181][web:182][web:184][web:191]

Accounting books and supporting documentation must be kept for six years from the last entry under the Commercial Code, while tax rules generally require four years; in practice businesses retain records for at least six years to satisfy both.[web:182][web:192]

Companies have broad obligations, while self-employed professionals (autónomos) follow simplified record books depending on tax regime, but VAT taxpayers regardless of form must keep registers for issued and received invoices, capital goods and intra‑community transactions.[web:179][web:182][web:183]

Purpose

The purpose of Spanish bookkeeping is to provide reliable financial information and documentation that support tax declarations, annual accounts and regulatory oversight.[web:182][web:187][web:190]

It converts legal and commercial obligations into structured books, records and annual financial statements, enabling audits and inspections.[web:181][web:182][web:192]

Primary Outcome

Accurate and timely Spanish bookkeeping with properly legalised books, up-to-date journal and inventory records, compliant annual accounts and documentation archived for at least six years.[web:181][web:182][web:187][web:190][web:191][web:192]

Regulatory & Operational Framework

Framework identifies the principal rule layers for Spanish bookkeeping.[web:181][web:182][web:183][web:187][web:190][web:192]

Commercial Code Requires entrepreneurs to keep books, correspondence, documentation and supporting documents organised for six years and defines mandatory accounting books.[web:181][web:183][web:192]
Spanish General Accounting Plan (PGC) Provides chart of accounts and principles, with variants for SMEs and micro‑enterprises and alignment with IFRS.[web:180][web:187][web:188][web:190]
Tax Regulations Define record books and retention obligations for various regimes, including VAT record books and specific rules for autónomos.[web:179][web:182][web:183][web:192]
Annual Accounts Requirements Directors must prepare annual accounts within three months of year‑end, have them approved within six months and file them within the following month.[web:182][web:187][web:190]
Process Flow

Process flow explains how bookkeeping moves from transactions to books and annual accounts in Spain.[web:181][web:182][web:183][web:187][web:189][web:190]

1. Configure Books and PGC Set up chart of accounts based on PGC and open the journal and Inventory/Annual Accounts book; configure general ledger in practice.[web:181][web:182][web:187][web:190]
2. Record Transactions in Journal Record transactions day by day, or quarterly totals backed by detailed concordant books, with date, accounts, amounts and description.[web:181][web:182]
3. Maintain Inventory and Annual Accounts Book Include opening balance sheet, quarterly trial balances, closing inventory and annual accounts.[web:181][web:182]
4. Legalise Books Legalise accounting books at the Mercantile Registry, typically telematically before the legal deadline.[web:181][web:191]
5. Annual Accounts Preparation Prepare annual accounts within three months after year‑end, approve them within six months and file within the following month.[web:182][web:187][web:190]
6. VAT and Tax Filings Use books and record registers to submit VAT self‑assessments, corporate tax and other obligations.[web:179][web:182][web:189][web:192]
7. Archiving Keep books and documentation organised and accessible for at least six years.[web:182][web:192]
Decision Tree

Decision tree simplifies threshold decisions in Spanish bookkeeping.[web:179][web:182][web:183][web:187][web:190]

  1. Identify whether the entity is a trading company (SL, SA, cooperative) or self‑employed professional.[web:182][web:191]
  2. Determine applicable tax regime (normal direct estimation, simplified direct estimation, objective estimation) for autónomos.[web:179][web:182][web:183]
  3. Define mandatory books: journal and Inventory/Annual Accounts for companies; specific record books for autónomos based on regime.[web:181][web:182][web:183]
  4. Assess whether annual accounts must be prepared, approved and filed, and whether audit thresholds are met.[web:182][web:187][web:190]
  5. Choose PGC variant (general, PGC‑SME, micro) that applies to the entity.[web:180][web:187][web:190]
  6. Plan retention and legalisation processes to ensure compliance with six‑year retention and Mercantile Registry requirements.[web:182][web:191][web:192]

FAQ

FAQ summarises recurring threshold questions on Spanish bookkeeping.[web:179][web:181][web:182][web:183][web:187][web:190][web:191][web:192]

Is It Compulsory to Keep Accounting Books? Yes. Trading companies must keep accounting books in accordance with the Commercial Code.[web:181][web:191]
Which Books Are Mandatory? Journal and Inventory/Annual Accounts book; general ledger is essential in practice.[web:181][web:182][web:184][web:191]
What Are Autónomo Record Obligations? They vary by regime: normal direct estimation mirrors company obligations, simplified uses sales/income, purchases/expenses and capital goods books, objective uses fewer books.[web:179][web:182][web:183]
How Long Must Records Be Kept? Six years from the last entry under Commercial Code; tax limitation is four years but six is recommended.[web:182][web:192]
When Must Annual Accounts Be Prepared and Filed? Prepared within three months after year‑end, approved within six months and filed within the following month.[web:182][web:187][web:190]
